About the Game

Sea Power: Naval Combat in the Missile Age is a real-time naval combat game created by Triassic Games, a multinational development team that features air, sea, and land combat with in-depth modeling of ships, aircraft, and land units, realistic graphics, accurate sensor behavior, and full control of units. The game features single missions, short campaigns, and a mission editor, with the ability to upload or download missions to or from the Steam community workshop.

The game was released on November 12, 2024 on Steam and as of the time of writing (August 2025) currently enjoys an 89% positive user rating.

Features

  • Real-time combat with the ability to pause at any time
  • Vast array of detailed 3D modeled ships, submarines, aircraft, and land units and installations
  • Weapons and sensors that are (mostly) accurately modeled in terms of capabilities (although this is a video game, not a simulator)
  • Play as units from the US, USSR, Japan, East and West Germany, Iran, and many other nations in a 1980's setting
  • Play scenarios based on real-life events, alternate history, or make up your own!
  • No arbitrary unit limits in missions; have as many units as you want (or until your CPU melts!)
  • Extensive mod support for both scenarios and missions and also new units
  • Easy accessibility to modify game files to your heart's content

FAQ

Q: Do I have to know much about naval combat to be able to play the game?

A: No, but if you know very little, your learning curve will probably look something like this. Some information is more practical, and that should be the emphasis. You don't for example need to know the difference between X- and P-band radars, but knowing when you should and shouldn't use your radars is important.

Q: How long does a mission usually take?

A: It depends on the mission and how much time dilation you use. More complicated missions with many ships and different objectives will take longer than a skirmish between a few ships, or a submarine duel if you use heavy time dilation. But I would say most missions take 1-1.5 hours even with some time compression. Fortunately saving and loading were added to the game, which makes it possible to stop and come back later, which is a life saver, especially for longer missions.

Q: How well does the game run?

A: The developers have been doing a good job optimizing the game in order to increase performance. That being said, during development they suggested having a computer with a dedicated graphics card, which I feel is appropriate. The game also calculates lots of variables in a short time, so having a decent CPU is also helpful. With a Ryzen 5 7600 CPU and a 3060 ti graphics card I get about 70 FPS starting the mission, dropping down the longer the game runs. It usually gets down to 15-25 FPS after about an hour of playing the same mission, regardless of how many units are in the game.

Q: How much does the game cost?

A: The game is $50 on Steam. It occasionally goes on sale, including during the Steam seasonal sales. You can check the price history here.

Q: Is there multiplayer in the game?

A: Currently there is no multiplayer. The developers have mentioned that they will consider adding it when the singleplayer elements are considered finished. There is little information as to whether multiplayer would be PvP or Co-op.

Q: What is the modding scene like? Are there any necessary mods?

A: The game is easily moddable and modders have been working since day 1 on different mods. A lot of them are just user-made scenarios that are mostly pretty good, some of them weren't the best but overall most of them are definitely worth your time. A lot of mods are also kitbashes of in-game units and custom models of units that aren't in the game. Personally I prefer more realistic mods in the game's time frame so haven't really dug into most of those mods. There aren't any necessary mods in my opinion, but some that are popular are nuclearstonk's NTU and Pact Enhanced Lethality mods, some mods that change aircraft carrier cycle times, and some mission packs like Ian's Pacific Strike '85.

Q: Are there any tutorials or walkthroughs that I can use to help me gain a better grasp of the game's concepts?

A: Yes! As of September 2025 the developers added a few tutorials in the game itself that goes over basic concets of how the game works and what you can do to make it easier to settle into the game, even if you don't know much about naval combat or are unfamiliar with naval strategy games.

Q: Are there any plans for DLC or expansions?

The developers mentioned in an AMA on the games first anniversary that they're considering a DLC for 1990's era weapons. They did not go into detail what exactly that would look like or what ships or aircraft would be a part of that. From their comments in the same AMA it seems that the Royal Navy will be a free addition to the game, and that other European navies, such as the French, West German, and Italian navies are also being considered for addition, again likely free updates to the game and not part of DLC, but that is unconfirmed.

Q: What navies are in the game?

The main navies are the US and Soviet navies, with ships from the East and West German, Japanese, Norweigan, Iranian, Libyan, Chinese, Japanese, and other navies. Many navies are also represented in name, operating ships built by another country, like Poland and Egypt operating Soviet ships, India operating old British ships, and so on. There are also many neutral and civilian ships and aircraft in the game from various countries, which can be used to make missions more interesting by forcing you to identify units before engaging them or risk suffering the consequences of attacking a neutral ship.

Q: What is the state of the game?

The game is still in Early Access after about a year after release. There has been a lot of additions to the game, both in new units and navies but also new quality of life and UI updates, as well as performance optimizations. The developers have stated that they are focused on improving the AI in the game and believe it will be a while before the dynamic campaign is added. As I mentioned in the performance question, there is still a lot of ground to cover in terms of optimization, but the game is very playable in its current state and a lot of fun. There has also been progress made in reducing micromanagement, although aircraft still require a lot of attention, and it gets to be a lot dealing with air assets along with maintaining situational awareness of the rest of your forces.

Q: What is the dynamic campaign supposed to be?

The dynamic campaign is still a WIP and so the final details are unknown but the general idea from what the developers have stated is that there will be a strategic map where you move forces around, and you or the enemy AI can initiate actions, bringing you into the mission mode where you fight with the relevant units in a certain area. It seems likely, but is not confirmed, that you will be able to create your own formations that you can use to initiate actions on your own. The campaign is currently planned to be based in the Atlantic Ocean between the US and USSR in the mid-1980s, with other campaigns in other areas and/or time periods possible but not planned.

Tips and Tricks

I have played the game for about 400 hours in total and also I am very interested in naval combat, both in WW1 and WW2 and the cold war. If you're struggling with the game, some of these tips and tricks can help you get a better grip on the game and have a more satisfying and enjoyable experience.

  • Be very careful with your emissions, both radar and sonar. You can be detected by the enemy a lot easier if you carelessly run your radars and sonars. With radar, if you have any aircraft, use those as radar stations before using your warships. Even helicopters are a huge help. If you have to use radar on warships, use them intermittently and not for long.
  • If using aircraft as radar stations, like AEW or AWACS planes, make sure they have a fighter escort. While less valuable than a ship, they are still important assets and will be priority targets for hostile aircraft.
  • Be careful with sonar as well. The slower your ships are going, the more effective their sonar will be. Use so-called sprint-and-drift tactics, where you speed in a certain direction for a few minutes before stopping and using active sonar. Helicopters are incredibly useful for ASW (Anti-submarine warfare), especially if they have dipping sonars.
  • Sonobouys are borderline necessary for ASW. Using sonobouys turns ASW from defensive to offensive in a heartbeat. Space them out, at least two nautical miles and no more than five away from each other, and deploy them both above and below the thermal layer.
  • If you're under attack by air or missile threats, turn on all your radars. The enemy already knows where you are, so being detected is no longer relevant, and you'll need to see any threats coming for you. That said, once you're confident its over, go back to EMCON (emissions control, aka turn off all your radars). Same thing with sonars and underwater threats like torpedoes.
  • If you're escorting valuable units like carriers, landing ships, attack aircraft, and the like, always keep your escorting units between the high value units and any hostile units, or at least where they're likely to come from. Defending high-value units is a lot easier if hostile units have to get through your escorts compared to your escorts having to chase hostile units who have a clear shot to the high-value units.
  • When launching a missile attack, the goal is to overwhelm and oversaturate the enemy's defenses. Knowing the capabilities of a warship's anti-air defenses helps in sizing up how many missiles you will need to oversaturate their defenses. Launching every anti-ship missile from a Soviet ship might seem unnecessary if they have 16-20 for example, but every missile makes the job of missile defense just a little harder. In the same vein, don't hesitate to launch all your aircraft with anti-ship missiles from an aircraft carrier or airfield at once.
  • Electronic warfare (EW) is stupidly powerful in both offensive and defensive roles. Missile attacks are much easier if an EW plane is there jamming hostile radars, which cuts down on the number of missiles you need. Also if your formations are under attack and you have a carrier present like most US carriers or the Soviet Orel, launching an EW aircraft and using to jam incoming missiles makes it less likely your ships will be hit. The US has the EA-6B and the Soviets have the Ko-45 EW and T-16P for EW, use them as much as you're able.
  • Knowing the capabilities of weapon systems is key. It's so much easier to make decisions on what to do if you know the capabilities of both your and hostile weapons. For example knowing the harpoon anti-ship missile's range will tell you how far you need to deploy fighters to stop aircraft from launching harpoons at your ships, or knowing the range for the SS-N-19 and which Soviet ships carry them will tell you how far away you need to be in order to keep those ships from launching against you.
  • Analyzing the behavior of unknown ships can give away their identity even if you haven't been able to classify them fully yet. For example knowing that merchant and other neutral ships or aircraft don't travel in ordered formations will immediately give away the existence of hostile units if you see several unknown ships traveling at the same speed and course in an ordered formation. Also knowing that most civilian ships don't travel very quickly, rarely moving more than 20 knots, makes identifying an unknown ship traveling at 30+ knots as hostile easy.
  • For unknown units, the game has the ability to request that they identify themselves. Turn on the radars of any friendly unit, then, with the friendly unit selected, press Ctrl, right click on the unidentified unit, and then scroll down to "Request: identify yourself." There are three possible responses: 1. they either acknowledge and you gain their information, 2. they refuse, or 3. they don't respond. In cases 2 and 3 you gain nothing, but if the unit is hostile they will almost always not respond. So if they refuse they are almost certainly neutral, although it might still be good to check.
  • On the bottom of the tactical map, there are drawing tools that make it so much easier to draw out distances between objects. The radial tool is particularly helpful. Use that to overlay weapon ranges to give yourself a visual guide of how close or far your units need to be to either engage with a weapon system or to ensure that hostile units can't engage your units with weapon systems they have.
  • Spread out your formations. It is tempting to keep all your ships close together, and while they do provide a high degree of mutual protection when close, they also make it easier for an enemy to detect and attack any high-value units. Spreading your units out will allow them to detect and engage hostiles before they get a chance at any valuable units. Especially with missile attacks, having a spread out formation that is also mutually supporting will present a gauntlet that provide more time to engage the missiles, instead of a quick screen that a small formation provides.

Glossary

A glossary of some of the terms and acronyms used

  • AEW - Airborne Early Warning: An aircraft-based radar system designed to detect enemy forces through radar and give friendly forces time to react to a threat. Synonymous with AWACS (Airborne Warning and Control System).
  • ASW - Anti-Submarine Warfare: The use of ships, aircraft, and submarines to hunt submarines, using torpedoes, depth charges, missiles, and sonobuoys.
  • CIWS - Close-In Weapons System: A weapons system that is designed to engage targets close to the ship it is mounted on. Considered the last line of defense against missiles, aircraft, and small craft. Early systems were 20 or 30 mm gatling guns, while modern systems include small missiles or lasers.
  • EW - Electronic Warfare: The use of electronic systems such as decoys or radar jamming to degrade an enemy's combat capability or to increase your own chances for success.
  • EMCON - Emissions Control: Aka "radio silence," preventing the use of radar or radio in order to prevent an enemy from discovering the existence of friendly forces through intercepting or detecting radio and radar use.
  • NATO - North Atlantic Treaty Organization: A military alliance between the USA, Canada, and several Western and Central European nations. Was aligned against the Warsaw Pact during the Cold War.
  • NBC - Nuclear-Biological-Chemical: A way of designing and protecting a ship and it's crew to make it more resistant to nuclear, biological, or chemical attack.
  • PLAN - People's Liberation Army Navy: The official name of the navy of communist China.
  • SAM - Surface-to-air Missile: A missile fired from a ship or a launcher on land that engages air targets, especially missiles and aircraft.
  • VLS - Vertical Launch System: A method of launching a missile vertically instead of using a rail launcher. A VLS usually allows for more missiles to be carried, has a much faster reaction time than a rail launcher, and allows all the ship's missiles to be available immediately.
  • Warsaw Pact: A defensive alliance between the Soviet Union and several Eastern European communist countries. Analogous to NATO, which they were aligned against.
